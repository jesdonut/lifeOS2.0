Roadmap

What Seratus can do now, what is being built, and what comes next. A personal project moves at its own pace.

Live
Calendar Month and week views. Events with 9 built-in categories and drag-and-drop between days. Daily spend tracked per category. Park unscheduled events to the notes sidebar for later. added May 18, 2026
Period tracker Year overview with 12 month calendars. Daily logger for flow, symptoms, and BBT. Cycle stats with EWMA-weighted predictions, fertile window, and ovulation estimates. Month detail panel with BBT chart and symptom summary. added May 19, 2026
Notes Collapsible sidebar available from every screen. Free-form notes and a parking lot for unscheduled events. added May 18, 2026
Import Apple Health XML parser: extracts period flow, spotting, BBT, discharge, ovulation, and contraceptive records. Parsed entirely on your device. added May 18, 2026
Settings Profile, timezone, calendar category editor, appearance, full data export and import, log out. added May 18, 2026
In progress
Calendar Year view with monthly overviews, goals, and age display. Week start day setting.
Finance Income and expense tracking. Country-adaptive fields for Japan: salary, health insurance, pension, resident tax. Daily calendar spending flows in automatically.
Next
Bank and savings Multi-country, multi-currency account balances. Fixed deposits, bonds, NISA (Japan), and pension contributions tracked in one place.
Currency and FX Lot tracking for JPY and IDR. Exchange rate logging and P&L in base currency.
Later
Import Clue JSON, Natural Cycles CSV, Flo JSON, and Seratus v1 JSON.
Period Birth control logging. Travel date adjustments for cycle predictions.
Calendar Recurring events. Goals with progress tracking.
v1, the original May 3, 2026

Built for one person. Public for a while, then quietly taken offline, though never fully gone. A single 2,500-line JS file that did everything: calendar, period tracker, finance, bank, savings, FX, notes. No build step, no framework, no concessions for anyone else. It had a certain static, web1 quality that felt right.

v2 started as the same thing. Then the question shifted: what if this could actually work for someone else too? Same principles, same data ownership, same no-nonsense stack. But built properly this time, with that possibility in mind.
